Genre · access to people of god-level

Who are the people of god-level

Definition
Those whose time cannot be bought retail at any price. The category is not defined by a single trait. It is defined by the absence of an ordinary channel of access.

Four sources of such status — the Thai case is the first source applied, the format extends to the other three.

01
Inherited or ritual power
Reigning monarchs. Heads of closed dynasties. Leaders of traditional religious schools.
02
Wealth at world scale
Forbes top-10 and top-50. Founders of global corporations. Old-capital family foundations whose time is either bound to a public role or insulated from outsiders.
03
Living cultural greatness
Nobel laureates and equivalents whose names became common nouns. Living legends of art, science and sport.
04
Concentration of knowledge
Keepers of unique traditions — the last living master of a craft, the sole speaker of a language, the heads of closed schools.

Dramaturgy · 8 phases

Anatomy of the ceremony

From the briefing one month before to the closing benediction. Durations are indicative. The key scene is the royal walkthrough — five small groups, a long audience for the Inner Circle, a short one for the Core, a ritual reading-aloud of the strongest written proposals from Observers.

PHASE 1 · T−30…T+45 MIN
Preparation & arrival
In advance of the ceremony all 150 receive a protocol briefing — dress code, forms of address, posture. Each gets a dossier of 4–6 candidate initiatives with metrics. Observers prepare a written proposal, one page. On the day, separate registration desks for the three tiers — visible to one another, so the structure is plain. Tea, ceremonial greeting. Observers deposit their proposals.
PHASE 2 · T+45 MIN…T+1H 15
Opening
A procession. The monarch enters in full regalia. The royal anthem. An opening address by His Majesty — 8 to 12 minutes, the year's frame and priorities. The shortlist of initiatives is announced, each defended briefly by a minister or expert.
PHASE 3 · T+1H 15…T+1H 30
Group formation
Five small groups of approximately thirty people each, deliberately mixed — every group contains Inner Circle, Core and Observers. No segregation by tier. Each group is matched to one shortlisted initiative and given an expert moderator, two notaries to record decisions, and a translator.
PHASE 4 · T+1H 30…T+3H
Council
Ninety minutes of structured discussion: problem · scale · concrete plan · budget · success metrics over twelve months. In parallel, royal aides triage Observer proposals and prepare thematic summaries for the monarch — one page per theme. Each group elects a spokesperson — most often, but not always, an Inner Circle member.
PHASE 5 · T+3H…T+5H · KEY SCENE
Royal walkthrough
The monarch visits each of the five groups in sequence. Within each:
  • Inner Circle (4 per group) — individual audience of 5–10 minutes in a separate niche of the group. Deep dialogue: what will you personally do beyond the contribution.
  • Core (16 per group) — short address, 1–3 minutes, in clusters of 4–5.
  • Observers (10 per group) — collective blessing. 3–5 strongest proposals from this group are read aloud by the royal aide while the monarch is present; the author rises. This is ritual recognition.
  • Between groups, summarised proposals are handed to the monarch in transitions.
PHASE 6 · T+5H…T+5H 30
Assembly
All return to the main hall. The five spokespersons present the final plan of their initiative — three minutes each. The monarch offers a brief comment and blessing for each.
PHASE 7 · T+5H 30…T+6H
Announcement
The Royal Charitable Council — advisors plus independent experts — announces the final allocation of funds. On the main screen: total raised, breakdown by initiative, committed KPIs and timelines. Donor names are listed, by participant choice — public or anonymous. A closing benediction.
PHASE 8 · T+6H…NIGHT
Continuation
Reception — Thai cuisine, classical music, traditional dance. Each participant receives a commemorative folio: personal invitation, photograph, and the Ehipas commitment to a twelve-month report on the funded initiatives.

Where the funds go · 6 initiatives

Six problems that cannot be postponed

Selected by acute severity, chronic underfunding and convertibility of money into measurable outcome. The participant chooses, before bidding, how the contribution is allocated across the six.

01
PM2.5 smoke in the north
Thousands of premature deaths each year in Chiang Mai, Mae Hong Son and the upper provinces — driven by agricultural burning. Children's lungs take the worst of it.
What we fund: subsidies for farmers who stop burning, satellite monitoring, school-grade air purifiers.
Approximately 50,000 ฿ per protected school
02
Child drowning
The leading cause of death for Thai children aged 1 to 14 — roughly one thousand children every year. Invisible in headlines, unmistakable in mortality statistics.
What we fund: free swimming courses through schools, fencing of unsafe water bodies, life jackets in fishing villages.
Approximately 1,500 ฿ per child trained
03
Stateless people
Over four hundred and eighty thousand residents — mostly hill tribe communities — without the right to legally work, receive healthcare or travel beyond their district.
What we fund: legal clinics, document workflows, naturalisation programmes.
Approximately 8,000 ฿ per citizenship case
04
An ageing population without infrastructure
By 2030 every fourth Thai citizen will be over sixty. The country is ageing faster than its care infrastructure is being built. Loneliness and untreated chronic conditions are the silent epidemic.
What we fund: day centres for the elderly across one hundred districts, mobile medical screening teams.
Approximately 1,200,000 ฿ per centre per year
05
Marine plastic
Thailand sits among the ten worst sources of ocean plastic in the world. Coastal fishing communities — the country's culinary identity — pay first.
What we fund: river-mouth waste interceptors, recycling infrastructure for fishing villages.
Approximately 15,000,000 ฿ per interceptor
06
Youth mental health
A rising rate of suicide among Thais aged fifteen to twenty-nine. Stigma keeps almost all of it underground. A generation is being lost in plain sight.
What we fund: school psychologists, a national helpline, accessible therapy.
Approximately 360,000 ฿ per school psychologist per year

For creators · what the organiser sees

What the creator of the experience receives

A figure of god-level status moving toward an audience requires infrastructure. Ehipas absorbs all of it, leaving the creator to focus on protocol and substance.

Ready-made infrastructure
Verification, auction, escrow, allocation, reporting — already operational. The creator does not build any of this from scratch.
The figure's dignity is protected
A ceremonial format with strict protocol — not a retail service. The figure remains in their status. The frame is a charitable convocation, not a commercial transaction.
Public legitimacy
Pre-declared expense commitments and KPIs neutralise the question "why is the figure offering time" before it can be asked.
Repeatable template
A format assembled once is preserved as a template on the platform and replayed yearly with the same toolkit. Not one-off heroism.
A buyer base, ready
A pre-verified audience of capital and curiosity from across the world — the creator does not hunt for guests one by one.
Reputation that compounds
Each successful ceremony raises both the creator's standing and the platform's. Creator and platform grow together, not at each other's expense.
Why this format only holds together on Ehipas

Six bottlenecks the platform removes

01
Verification of participants
A platform with multi-level identity and solvency checks. Without it the wrong people reach the monarch — with the wrong protocol, the wrong intent, or simply unprepared.
02
Simultaneous flash auction
A fixed Ehipas mechanism, validated across one thousand simulation runs — gives +16.6% over an English auction. Every additional baht goes to the initiatives.
03
Escrow until the fact of the ceremony
Funds are held by the platform until the Royal Household confirms the event has taken place. Released directly to the initiatives — no intermediaries.
04
Allocation locked before the bid
Each participant sees the exact distribution across the six initiatives and the committed outcomes before paying. No post-fact substitution.
05
Annual reporting
Structured twelve-month reports to every participant on the funded initiatives — wired into the ticket terms. Not a press release a month later.
06
A Thai platform with a repeatable format
A regulatory track inside Thailand. The format is templated for annual repetition with the same toolkit — not the one-off heroism of the Royal Household.
